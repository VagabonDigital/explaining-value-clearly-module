Module Overview

What This Module Builds

Explaining value is not about listing features. It is about helping the client understand why your offer matters for the specific problem they described.

In this module

We’ll practise how to explain value in a way that is clear, specific, and connected to the client’s situation — not generic, not feature-heavy, and not vague.

The goal is not to sound impressive. The goal is to help the client see why your offer is relevant to the problem they described.

Typical path

You’ll build
  • Clearer value explanations connected to the client’s problem
  • Less feature-heavy and generic language
  • Stronger business impact statements

  • Smoother transitions from explanation to next step
  • More confidence when explaining offers and recommendations

Skill Path

A clear value explanation is built one layer at a time.

A strong value explanation is not a single sentence. It is a sequence — start with the problem, explain why it matters, connect to an outcome, add simple proof, and move forward.

1
Start from the problem
Begin with what the client is trying to solve, not what your product does.
2
Show why it matters
Name the impact, cost, delay, or risk behind the problem so it feels worth solving.
3
Translate features into value
Turn product details into business outcomes the client actually cares about.
4
Make the value specific
Replace vague words like “better” and “faster” with concrete improvements.
5
Add proof without overloading
Support the value with a simple example or result — not a full data presentation.
6
Move toward the next step
Value should lead somewhere. End the explanation with a clear, useful action.

Core Framework

The Value Explanation Framework

When you explain value, it is easy to drift into features, vague language, or over-explanation. This structure gives you a clear path.

1
Problem
Name the client’s problem.
“The issue you described is…”
2
Impact
Explain why the problem matters.
“That matters because…”
3
Value
Connect your offer to a specific improvement.
“The value here is…”
4
Proof
Add a simple example, result, or credibility point.
“For example…”
5
Next Step
Move from explanation to a clear, useful action.
“A useful next step would be…”
Framework note
You do not need to use every step every time. The framework gives you a reliable path when you need to explain value clearly and specifically.
RememberProblem→Impact→Value→Proof→Next Step
Mini Example — Full Sequence
Client
“We’re comparing a few options, but it’s hard to know which one fits best.”
Response
“The issue you described is that your team needs a clearer way to compare options before deciding. That matters because without a clear comparison, the decision can become slow or based on surface-level differences. The value here is making the trade-offs easier to understand so your team can choose the option that fits your priorities. For example, we can compare implementation time, support, and ease of use side by side. A useful next step would be to review those options together.”

Common Weak Patterns

Most value explanations fail for one of these reasons. Tap a card to see what goes wrong and what to do instead.

Tap any card to expand it.

Pattern 1
“We have dashboards, automation, integrations, and reporting.”
Weak pattern: Feature dump

Listing features without connecting them to an outcome. The client hears what the product does, not why it matters for them.

Better move:

“The reporting helps your team spot issues earlier and make decisions with better information.”

Translate the feature into an outcome
Pattern 2
“It saves time and improves efficiency.”
Weak pattern: Too generic

Vague benefit language that could describe almost anything. The client cannot picture what actually changes for them.

Better move:

“This helps your team compare the options faster so the decision does not get stuck.”

Connect the value to the specific problem
Pattern 3
“This is definitely the best solution on the market.”
Weak pattern: Overclaiming

Strong claims without evidence. This can make the client more skeptical, not more convinced.

Better move:

“Based on what you described, the most relevant value is helping your team compare the options more clearly before deciding.”

Make the value relevant, not exaggerated
Pattern 4
“It connects through our enterprise API layer.”
Weak pattern: Too technical

Technical detail without explaining what it helps the client do. Most clients do not care how it works — they care what changes.

Better move:

“That means your team does not have to enter the same information twice — it connects to the tools you already use.”

Explain what the detail actually helps them do
Pattern 5
“We have a lot of case studies and data.”
Weak pattern: Proof overload

Mentioning evidence without using it clearly. Too much data makes the explanation heavy and hard to follow.

Better move:

“For example, the onboarding guide can help new people understand the process without needing extra support every time.”

Use one focused example instead
Pattern 6
“So yes, that’s basically what we do.”
Weak pattern: No next step

The explanation ends but goes nowhere. Value without a next step loses momentum quickly.

Better move:

“If that sounds relevant, a useful next step would be to compare the options against your main priorities.”

Connect the explanation to a clear action

Skill 1 of 6

Start From the Problem

Key Move: Begin with what the client is trying to solve — not what your product does.
Why this matters
If you start with your product, the value explanation can feel generic. Starting from the client’s problem makes it easier for them to understand why the offer is relevant — because they recognise what you are describing.
Their problem comes before your solution.
Useful language
“The issue you described is…”
“You mentioned that…”
“The problem this is designed to address is…”
“From what you said, the main challenge is…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Our platform helps with decision-making and comparison.”
Stronger
“The issue you described is that your team is comparing a few options but finding it hard to know which one fits best. That is the problem this is designed to help with.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We’re comparing a few options but it’s hard to know which one fits best.”
Model response
“The problem you described is that your team has options to compare but does not yet have a clear way to decide which one fits your priorities.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“We’re looking at a few options but haven’t decided yet.”
Task: Restate the client’s situation as a problem in your own words before moving to any solution. Use: “The issue you described is…”
“We need to make a decision soon but we’re not sure which direction is right.”
Task: Open a value explanation by naming the client’s problem first — before mentioning anything about your offer.
“I don’t have time to explain everything — just tell me what it does.”
Challenge: The client is impatient. Start from their problem anyway — but do it in one sentence.
Quick Recap
The client needs to recognise the problem before they can understand the value.
“The issue you described is…”
Skill 2 of 6

Show Why It Matters

Key Move: Explain the impact of the problem before explaining the solution.
Why this matters
A problem becomes more convincing when the client understands the cost, delay, risk, or missed opportunity behind it. Impact turns a problem into a reason to act.
Impact makes value feel real.
Useful language
“That matters because…”
“The impact is…”
“The risk is that…”
“Over time, that can create…”
“That can slow down…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“It’s hard to compare the options.”
Stronger
“That matters because without a clear way to compare, the decision can slow down or end up based on surface-level differences rather than what actually fits your priorities.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We have a few options on the table but the decision keeps getting delayed.”
Model response
“That matters because when a decision keeps getting delayed, the team loses time it could spend on implementation — and the longer it takes, the harder it can be to build alignment around any option.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
Problem: “We’re not sure which option to go with.”
Task: Add one sentence explaining why this matters as a business problem. Use: “That matters because…”
“We’re worried about how long the rollout will take.”
Task: Name the concern, then explain the business impact. What does implementation risk actually cost the team?
“I know it’s not ideal, but it’s not a huge problem.”
Challenge: The client is minimising the issue. Explain the impact without making them feel defensive or wrong.
Quick Recap
A problem without impact is just a complaint. Impact turns a problem into a reason to act.
“That matters because…”
Skill 3 of 6

Translate Features Into Value

Key Move: Connect product features to outcomes the client actually cares about.
Why this matters
Clients do not always care about features by themselves. They care about what those features help them improve, avoid, reduce, or achieve. A feature describes what a product does. Value explains why it matters.
A feature says what it is. Value says why it matters.
Useful language
“The value of that is…”
“What that means in practice is…”
“This helps your team…”
“Instead of…, your team can…”
“This is useful because…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“It has a reporting dashboard and built-in analytics.”
Stronger
“The value is that your team can see the comparison in one place, which makes it easier to identify which option fits your priorities before deciding.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“What does the reporting dashboard actually help us do?”
Model response
“What that means in practice is your team can compare the options side by side in one view, so the decision does not rely on switching between documents or trying to remember what each option said.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
Feature: “We provide a side-by-side comparison view across all options.”
Task: Translate this feature into a value statement. Use: “The value of that is…” or “What that means in practice is…”
“What features does it have?”
Task: The client asked about features. Instead of listing them, translate the two or three most relevant into outcomes the client would care about.
“Just give me the list of features so I can compare.”
Challenge: The client only wants features. Acknowledge the request, then connect the most relevant feature to a specific outcome before continuing.
Quick Recap
Every feature should have an answer to: what does this help the client actually do?
“What that means in practice is…”
Skill 4 of 6

Make the Value Specific

Key Move: Replace vague value language with concrete, specific language.
Why this matters
Phrases like “better,” “faster,” and “more efficient” only work if the client knows exactly what they refer to. Specific value language is easier to understand, easier to believe, and harder to dismiss.
Specific beats impressive.
Useful language
“More specifically…”
“In your case, that would mean…”
“The practical improvement would be…”
“For your team, the value would be…”
“That would mean fewer…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“This will make onboarding easier and faster for your team.”
Stronger
“More specifically, new team members can follow the process independently from day one, without needing someone to walk them through it each time.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“So it just makes onboarding easier?”
Model response
“More specifically, it means new team members can get started without waiting for someone to explain the process to them — the guidance is built in.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
Vague: “It will make adoption easier for your team.”
Task: Rewrite this as a specific value statement. Replace “easier” with a concrete improvement.

Use: “More specifically…” or “In your case, that would mean…”
“We need something the whole team can use, not just the technical people.”
Task: Acknowledge the goal, then make the value specific. What concrete change would wider adoption actually mean for the team?
“Every vendor says the same thing. What specifically makes this different?”
Challenge: The client is skeptical of generic claims. Respond with a specific value statement connected to their situation — not a generic comparison.
Quick Recap
Vague value claims are forgettable. Specific value claims are believable.
“More specifically… In your case, that would mean…”
Skill 5 of 6

Add Proof Without Overloading

Key Move: Support the value with a simple example, result, or credibility point — not a data dump.
Why this matters
Proof makes a value explanation more credible, but too much proof makes it heavy, defensive, or hard to follow. The goal is to use just enough to support the value — not to overwhelm the client with evidence.
One clear example beats a long list of data.
Useful language
“For example…”
“Teams often use this to…”
“A common example is…”
“In practice, this usually means…”
“One way this shows up is…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“We have extensive case studies and a 97% satisfaction rating.”
Stronger
“For example, teams often use the comparison summary in the first meeting to align quickly on which option fits their priorities.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“How do other teams actually use it?”
Model response
“For example, teams often use the support resources during the first few weeks to get everyone up to speed without needing to run separate training sessions.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
Value: “The value is that your team can compare the options clearly before deciding.”
Task: Add one short proof point to this value statement. Use: “For example…” or “Teams often use this to…”
“I’m not convinced this would really make a difference for us.”
Task: The client is skeptical. Add a simple, relevant proof point that supports the value — without turning it into a sales pitch.
“Can you give me specific numbers or results?”
Challenge: The client wants hard evidence. Respond honestly and helpfully — with the most specific proof you have — without over-promising or making up numbers.
Quick Recap
Use just enough proof to make the value credible. One clear example is usually enough.
“For example, teams often use this to…”
Skill 6 of 6

Move Toward the Next Step

Key Move: End the value explanation with a clear, useful next action.
Why this matters
A good value explanation can still fail if the conversation ends vaguely. Value should lead somewhere useful. If the conversation ends with “So yes, that’s what it does,” momentum disappears.
Value without a next step loses momentum.
Useful language
“A useful next step would be…”
“If that sounds relevant…”
“Would it make sense to…?”
“The next thing to look at would be…”
“We could look at how this fits your process.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“So yes, that’s the value. Anyway — let me know what you think.”
Stronger
“If that sounds relevant, a useful next step would be to look at how this fits your current process.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“That makes sense.”
Model response
“Good. A useful next step would be to review the options against your main priorities so we can identify which one fits best.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“The value is that your team can compare the options clearly and choose the one that fits best.”
Task: Add a next step to this value statement. Choose one that feels natural.

Use: “A useful next step would be…” or “Would it make sense to…?”
“A useful next step would be to review the options against your main priorities.”
“Would it make sense to compare each option against what matters most to your team?”
“If that sounds relevant, the next step would be to check how each option fits your timeline and budget.”
“I can see how that could be useful.”
Task: The client acknowledged the value but did not commit. Propose a next step that feels natural and low-pressure.
“I need to check with the team before we move forward.”
Challenge: The client is pausing. Suggest a next step that supports their process and keeps things moving without pressuring them.
Quick Recap
Value should lead somewhere. Always end the explanation with a clear and useful next action.
“A useful next step would be…”

Practice & Reference

What Good Looks Like

A strong value explanation does not need to be long. It needs to be specific, connected, and clear.

1
Starts from the client’s problem
“The issue you described is…” — The explanation begins with something the client recognises, not with a product description.
2
Explains why the problem matters
“That matters because…” — The impact gives the problem weight and turns it into a reason to act.
3
Connects to a specific outcome
“The value here is…” — The explanation says what changes for the client, not just what the product does.
4
Avoids feature dumping
Features are only mentioned when they connect directly to an outcome the client cares about.
5
Moves toward a next step
“A useful next step would be…” — The explanation leads somewhere, rather than just ending.
Strong response checklist

A strong value explanation usually does at least three of these:

starts from the client’s problem
explains why the problem matters
avoids feature dumping
uses specific business language
adds a simple proof point
suggests a clear next step
